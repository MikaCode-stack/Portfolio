Cluster 0 — Foundations

Scouting Essentials

Module
+
Youth-Led Scouting

Young people are placed at the centre of decision-making within the troop. Through the Patrol Leaders' Council, Scouts contribute to programme planning, evaluations and troop improvements. Leaders provide guidance while allowing young people to progressively take ownership of their Scouting experience.

Learning by Doing

Activities are designed around practical experiences rather than classroom teaching. Hikes, camps, pioneering projects, governance challenges, heritage visits and outdoor activities allow Scouts to learn through direct participation and reflection.

Safety & Wellbeing

The wellbeing of young people remains a priority in all activities. Risk assessments, first aid preparation, adult supervision and Safe from Harm principles are integrated into meetings, camps and expeditions to ensure a safe and supportive environment.

Inclusion & Accessibility

Consistent with the principles of Scouting, the troop welcomes young people regardless of background, religion or personal circumstances. Activities are adapted where required to encourage participation and ensure every member feels valued and respected.

Community Engagement

Service remains an essential part of the programme. Scouts are encouraged to contribute positively to their communities through environmental initiatives, district projects, heritage activities and acts of service.

Continuous Improvement

Evaluations are conducted regularly through Patrol Leaders' Council meetings and leader discussions. Feedback from Scouts is used to improve future programmes and maintain activities that remain relevant and engaging.

Supporting Documents

Programme plans, risk assessments, PLC minutes, camp reports, activity evaluations, attendance sheets and Safe from Harm documentation.

Section Operations

Module
+
Scout Troop Structure

The Scout Troop consists of 34 Scouts organised into five patrols. Each patrol is led by a Patrol Leader and Assistant Patrol Leader who are responsible for coordinating activities, communication and patrol development.

Patrol Leaders' Council

The Patrol Leaders' Council consists of five Patrol Leaders, five Assistant Patrol Leaders, a Senior Patrol Leader and an Assistant Senior Patrol Leader. The PLC meets regularly to evaluate activities, discuss troop matters and contribute to programme planning.

Youth Participation

As the Patrol Leaders' Council was recently restructured, emphasis is currently placed on leadership development and empowering young leaders to progressively assume greater responsibilities within the troop.

Managers & Supporters

Module
+
La Maîtrise

The Group Scouters' Meeting serves as the principal decision-making and coordination body of the group. Leaders from all sections meet regularly to discuss programme delivery, group development, safeguarding concerns and upcoming activities.

Leadership Team

The group currently consists of approximately 14 adult leaders across the Cub, Scout and Venture sections. Meetings are generally held once or twice per week depending on operational requirements.

Decision-Making Process

Consensus is the preferred method of decision-making. Voting is used only when consensus cannot be reached. The Group Scout Leader chairs meetings with support from the Assistant Group Scout Leader, Secretary and Treasurer.

Annual General Meeting

The AGM is normally held at the beginning of each year with the participation of parents, the sponsoring authority and supporters. Financial reports, annual plans and budgets are presented while stakeholders contribute to discussions on the future direction of the group.

Stakeholder Engagement

Parents, former Scouts, district leadership and the sponsoring authority provide valuable support through volunteering, fundraising, logistics and strategic guidance for the group.

Adult Support & Development

Responsibilities are distributed among leaders to encourage ownership, improve retention and provide opportunities for personal development. New leaders are supported through mentoring and gradual integration into group operations.

Supporting Documents

AGM reports, meeting minutes, organisational charts, budgets, annual calendars and committee records.

Safety & Wellbeing

Module
+
Safe from Harm Implementation
  • Safe from Harm briefings conducted during camps and major activities.
  • Listening Ears identified during residential experiences.
  • Bullying, exclusion and inappropriate behaviour addressed immediately.
  • Young people encouraged to raise concerns in confidence.
  • Respectful behaviour standards communicated to all participants.
Health & First Aid
  • First aid kits available during meetings, hikes and camps.
  • Medical information collected before residential activities.
  • Emergency contacts maintained and accessible.
  • Qualified first aiders identified for major events.
  • Emergency procedures reviewed before activities.
Risk Management
  • Risk assessments completed before major activities.
  • Hazards identified and control measures documented.
  • Activity leaders briefed on mitigation measures.
  • Dynamic risk assessments conducted when conditions change.

Module

Personal Information

Personal information collected from members and parents is used solely for Scouting purposes. Access is restricted to authorised leaders where operationally necessary.

Medical information is collected before camps and major activities and handled confidentially to ensure the safety and wellbeing of participants.

Cluster 1 — Growing & Delivering

Changes in Scouting

Module
+
58+ Years of Scouting

The 16th Scout Group has existed for more than 58 years. Its continuity shows the ability of the group to adapt to new realities while preserving its traditions, identity and values.

Scouting Online

During the COVID-19 pandemic, the group adapted by using online Scouting methods to maintain engagement, communication and learning despite physical restrictions.

Modern Programme Approach

The group continues to evolve by integrating more youth participation, practical activities, digital tools, inclusive practices and programme themes that reflect the interests and needs of today’s young people.

Supporting Documents

Evidence can include historical group records, photos, online activity screenshots, programme plans, and testimonies from leaders.

Programme Planning

Module
+
1. Start-Off

At the end of August / early September, we launched the Governance Challenge with an introductory session on governance, leadership and decision-making.

2. MSA Organigram

Scouts were introduced to the main decision-making bodies within the Mauritius Scout Association. They also interviewed group leaders to learn their names, roles and responsibilities.

3. Historical Expedition

Near the end of September, Scouts visited key historical sites in Port Louis, including the Citadel, to understand their importance, heritage value and connection to civic life.

4. Parliament Visit

Earlier this year, Scouts visited the National Parliament during National Heritage Day. This gave them the opportunity to discover the parliamentary chamber and better understand national governance.

5. Final Evaluation

Scouts had to present concrete evidence, such as photos proving their participation, and demonstrate a basic understanding of governance and why it is necessary within different institutions.

6. Lessons Learned

This programme showed that Scouts understand governance better when theory is combined with practical exploration, interviews, site visits and personal reflection.

Supporting Documents

Evidence can include programme plan, photos, visit records, activity instructions, Scout presentations, attendance list and evaluation notes.

Registration & Records

Administration includes keeping accurate records of members, attendance, activities and payments where applicable. This helps the group plan safely and maintain proper follow-up.

Communication

Clear communication with parents, leaders and young people is essential. This may include letters, WhatsApp messages, emails, posters and reminders before activities.

Event Documentation

For activities and camps, administrative preparation includes forms, permission letters, budgets, transport arrangements, programmes, emergency contacts and supporting documents.

Good administration makes activities safer, clearer and more professional. It also builds trust with parents and stakeholders because responsibilities, information and expectations are properly documented.

Scouting for All

Module
+
Religious Inclusion

The 16th Scout Group has strong Catholic roots, but the group has evolved as members from different religious backgrounds have joined. We aim to remain respectful of our traditions while ensuring that all young people feel welcomed and included.

Supporting Different Needs

Some parents have shared that their children may show autistic behaviours or require additional support. The group welcomes these profiles and benefits from adult leaders, especially in the Cub section, who have teaching experience and better understanding of different learning needs.

Safe from Harm Culture

Safe from Harm training has helped us better understand inclusion, safeguarding and respectful behaviour. Bullying, exclusion and discrimination are not tolerated, and we work to create a peaceful and supportive environment for all members.

Growing the Section

Module
+
Announcements During Mass

Announcements during weekly mass help attract potential new members. Many families attending church are interested in activities that support the development of their children, making this an effective recruitment channel.

State House Kermesse

Although this was a national initiative, participation in the State House Kermesse helped promote Scouting and led to the recruitment of two new adults and two new young members within the section.

Responsibility Sharing

To retain adults, responsibilities are shared among leaders so that everyone feels useful, valued and involved in the life of the group. This strengthens ownership and commitment.

Team-Building Activities

Frequent team-building activities, such as hikes and end-of-year gatherings, help maintain morale, improve coordination and create a family-like atmosphere among adult volunteers.

Cluster 2 — Supporting Young People

Supporting Young People

Module
+
Empowering Youth Through Participation

I believe that young people develop best when they are trusted with responsibility and supported in making decisions that affect them. My approach to youth support is based on meaningful participation, where young people are not only consulted but also encouraged to plan, lead, evaluate and improve their own Scouting experience.

Within my Scout Group, I have supported the rebuilding of the Patrol Leaders’ Council to strengthen youth involvement in programme planning and decision-making. I have also been a support to the Venture Executive Committee by guiding members, helping them structure their ideas, and encouraging them to take ownership of their section activities.

At district and regional level, my experience as President of the District Youth Scout Council of Port Louis and as Youth Representative for the Africa Scout Region has broadened my perspective on youth participation. It has reinforced my belief that young people need real platforms, proper guidance and trust in order to contribute meaningfully to decisions that affect them.

Supporting Documents

Evidence can include PLC meeting notes, Venture Executive Committee records, DYSF materials, youth consultation notes, photos, and feedback from young members.

Positive Behaviour

Module
+
Mentorship Approach

Positive behaviour in our Scout Group is encouraged through mentorship and individual support. Leaders take time to guide young members, understand their challenges, and help them improve through discussion rather than only correction.

Patrol Reward & Recognition

The group has implemented a Patrol Reward and Recognition System to encourage teamwork, discipline, attendance, participation and good conduct. This creates healthy motivation while reinforcing the values expected from Scouts.

Uniform Inspections

Frequent uniform inspections are carried out to encourage discipline, pride and personal responsibility. The aim is not only to correct mistakes but to help young members understand the importance of representing the Movement properly.

Reflection

These approaches have shown that positive behaviour is more sustainable when young people feel guided, recognised and respected. Mentorship, recognition and clear expectations help create a stronger sense of belonging and accountability.

Supporting Documents

Evidence can include patrol reward records, uniform inspection sheets, photos, attendance records, mentorship notes, and activity reports.

Residential Experiences

Module
+
Start-Up

The first stage of organising a residential experience is to define the camp objectives, theme, target participants, expected outcomes and proposed location. This ensures that the camp has a clear educational purpose and is not only a logistical activity.

Pre-Camp Planning

Pre-camp planning includes collecting information on feasibility, preparing a risk assessment, drafting the budget, building the programme, and preparing proposals or requests for approval from relevant stakeholders.

Logistics & Permissions

This stage includes confirming the venue, arranging transport, seeking required permissions, preparing the menu, checking available equipment, identifying missing materials, and ensuring that activities can be delivered safely and effectively.

Delivery Preparation

Before the residential experience, the organising team finalises workshops, prepares activity materials, confirms facilitators, arranges transport, assigns responsibilities, and ensures first aid and Safe from Harm measures are in place.

During the Camp

During the camp, the team supervises participants, coordinates with the Chef de Camp, monitors safety, follows up with the kitchen team, supports facilitators, and ensures that participants and volunteers remain physically and emotionally safe.

Report & Evaluation

After the camp, feedback is collected from participants, volunteers and organisers. This helps evaluate the quality of the programme, identify challenges, and improve the planning of future residential experiences.

Supporting Documents

Evidence can include camp proposal, budget, programme, transport plan, menu, risk assessment, permission letters, evaluation forms, attendance list, and photos.

Risk Management

Module
+
Risk Assessment Process

Risk management begins before the activity by identifying possible hazards, assessing the likelihood and impact of each risk, and deciding on control measures. This helps ensure that activities are delivered safely while still allowing young people to experience challenge and adventure.

Main Areas Considered
  • Participant safety and supervision
  • Venue and environmental risks
  • Transport and movement
  • Food, hygiene and hydration
  • First aid and emergency response
  • Safe from Harm procedures
Real Example

For residential activities and district-level events, risk assessment is used to identify potential issues before the activity begins. This includes checking the venue, planning emergency contacts, assigning responsibilities, and ensuring that safety measures are understood by the organising team.

Reflection

Risk assessment has taught me that safety planning should not be treated as paperwork only. It is a practical tool that helps leaders anticipate problems, protect participants, and respond more confidently during an activity.

International Scouting

Module
+
Online Application

Participation in international Scouting events usually begins with an application process. Young people need to understand the event requirements, eligibility criteria, deadlines and documents required before applying.

Fundraising

International events often require financial preparation. Fundraising can help make participation more accessible by reducing the cost burden on young people and their families.

Contingent Training

Before attending an international event, participants should receive preparation through contingent training. This may include cultural awareness, travel expectations, safety briefings, event objectives and team-building activities.

Youth Preparation

Young people should be supported to understand their role as representatives of their group, association and country. This includes behaviour expectations, communication, responsibility and openness to learning from other cultures.

Reflection

International Scouting gives young people the opportunity to develop global citizenship, confidence and intercultural understanding. Proper preparation ensures that they benefit fully from the experience.

Lessons Learned

Young people need early guidance, clear information and financial planning support to access international opportunities successfully.

Supporting Documents

Evidence can include event invitations, application forms, fundraising plans, training programmes, travel preparation documents, photos and participant reflections.

Cluster 3 — Leadership & Learning

Leadership Skills

Module
+
Autocratic Leadership

During my mandate as President of the District Youth Scout Council, I sometimes had to make quick decisions when time was limited. This was mainly for minor operational matters such as drafting emails, preparing communication content, or validating marketing material. I used this approach carefully, only when immediate action was needed and when the decision did not affect the wider team’s direction.

Democratic Leadership

Through the Patrol Leaders’ Council, we consult young members before planning and organising activities. For example, Patrol Leaders expressed their interest in having more expeditions and outdoor experiences. As a result, this year we organised two field trips, participated in one district-level camp, and started planning a mini-camp before our annual group camp.

Laissez-faire Leadership

One lesson I learned as DYSC President is that leadership also means knowing when to step back. During the District Youth Scout Forum, I delegated specific responsibilities to my team, including the preparation and facilitation of SDG-related activities. Once the team clearly understood the objectives, they were able to deliver independently, allowing me to trust their ownership and focus on overall coordination.

Working with Adults

Module
+
Issue 1 — Recognition within the Group

During one of our La Maîtrise meetings, a situation arose where one leader felt that their contribution was not being sufficiently recognised within the group. This affected their motivation and participation during discussions.

Solution 1 — Listening and Shared Responsibility

We acknowledged the concern and created space for the leader to express their views. To help them feel more included, we encouraged greater participation in meetings and assigned more meaningful responsibilities. This helped rebuild confidence and improved engagement within the leadership team.

Issue 2 — Understanding the Purpose of DYSF

In the build-up to the District Youth Scout Forum, some Scouters, leaders and parents did not fully understand the purpose of the forum or the importance of youth participation within the district.

Solution 2 — Clear Communication

Through consistent communication, explanatory posts, videos and direct discussions, we helped clarify the objectives and benefits of the forum. This approach reduced doubts, increased trust, and encouraged better participation from both young people and adults.

Supporting Documents

Evidence can include meeting notes, DYSF communication materials, parent information posts, videos, invitation letters, and participation records.

Instructing Practical Skills

Module
+
Practical Skill Selected

The practical skill selected was linked to the Pioneer Activity Badge. Scouts were introduced to basic pioneering concepts, including knots, teamwork, planning, and simple gadget construction.

Preparation

Before the activity, the leadership team identified the skills to be demonstrated, prepared the necessary materials, and planned the session so that Scouts could first observe the technique and then practise it in small groups.

Animation

The activity was delivered through a practical, hands-on method. Scouts were guided step by step, then given the opportunity to apply the skill themselves through a mini team challenge, encouraging both learning and collaboration.

Evaluation

Scouts were assessed through observation and demonstration. Leaders checked whether they could correctly apply the technique, work safely, and explain the purpose of the skill they had learned.

Reflection

This activity showed that young people learn practical skills more effectively when they are actively involved rather than only listening to instructions.

Lessons Learned

For future sessions, more time should be allocated for practice and peer learning, especially for Scouts who need additional support before being assessed.

Supporting Documents

Evidence can include activity plans, badge requirements, photos of the practical activity, assessment notes, and feedback from leaders.

Assessing Learning

Module
+
Outdoor Evaluation — Hiking

A recent trip to Dauguet allowed us to assess Scouts working towards their Orienteering Activity Badge. They were given a compass and asked to guide their team through the forest using cardinal directions.

On-the-Field Assessment

The assessment was conducted in a real outdoor setting rather than through theory only. This helped leaders observe whether Scouts could apply their knowledge practically, communicate with their patrol, and make decisions during the activity.

Evidence Gathering

Evidence was gathered through direct observation, leader notes, team performance, and how well Scouts completed the route while using the compass correctly.

Learning Standard

The expected standard was that Scouts could identify directions, use a compass safely and correctly, and support their team during the orientation activity.

Reflection

This assessment showed that outdoor skills are best evaluated in real situations where young people can demonstrate confidence, teamwork and practical understanding.

Lessons Learned

Future assessments should include a short briefing before the activity and a reflection session afterwards to help Scouts better understand what they achieved and what they can improve.

Supporting Documents

Evidence can include route plans, photos, badge records, assessment notes, and attendance sheets.

Traditional Teaching

Resource Person Intervention

A recent visit to the Rajiv Gandhi Science Centre allowed the group to benefit from qualified facilitators for the Techno Challenge. This was especially useful for topics such as artificial intelligence and science, where external expertise strengthened the learning experience.

Practical Activities

Interactive Outdoor Games

Mini Quiz

For Founder’s Day, we organised a quiz on the life of Baden-Powell. This made the learning process more interactive and encouraged members to discover the founder’s life in a fun and participative way.

This can be done through short questions, group discussion, recap activities or asking members to explain the key points in their own words.
